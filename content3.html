The Cheese

Depending on the style of cheese we are making, we cool the milk to 70 or 88 degrees. At this point, specific cultures are added depending on the style of cheese we are making. After the cultures have been added, we wait approximately 1 hour, gently stirring the curd, and slowly the cultures active and populating in the milk. After an hour, we add an enzyme called rennet, the amount again dependent upon cheese. The more rennet will cause the milk to coagulate faster. For our soft fresh style cheese, we may add only a few drops, whereas in a harder style we will add up to multiple teaspoons to help it coagulate quicker.

In the soft goat cheese the cultures are converting the lactose into lactic acid and a little bit of rennet helps with coagulation, resulting a curd resembling soft yogurt after 18 hours. At this point we ladle the curd into cheese cloth to hang for two more days, allowing they whey to drain from the curds leaving us the soft cheese. Then we add salt and other ingredients like herbs, garlic, smoked jalapeno and candied ginger to create about 8 varieties of the soft cheese also called chevre. The cheeses is put into a sausague press and a long tube of cheese is pressed out. It is then chilled in long logs to make it easier to cut and be packaged into retail sizes. We also will bulk package this cheese in five pound bags for restaurant use.

If we are making a hard style, more rennet is used to coagulate the milk in forty minutes versus 18 hours. This is also known as rennet coagulation versus lactic acid coagulation. After the forty minutes we use stainless steel cheese harps with vertical and horizontal wires to cut the curd, which is now firm like tofu into 3/8ths cubes. Over the next hour we heat the curds up from 88 to 102 degrees while stirring. This is called cooking the curds, allowing them to release whey and increases the lactic acid which helps in the aging process of the cheese. After the hour we remove all the whey from the vat and are left with about 80% of the volume of the milk in whey leaving us a curd pack of about ten inches. The curd is scooped out into plastic micro-perforated cheese moulds. One mould is stacked on top of another to give a light pressing allowing more whey to be extracted. After twelve hours, we removed the curd from the moulds and dry salt the outside of the cheese. After three or four days of drying, which includes flipping the cheese every other day, the cheeses are put in the cheese cave on ash boards and age for 12 months to two years. As the cheese ages and dries it gains sharpness. We sell this style in whole wheels to cheese shops and restaurants and cut into smaller sizes for retail shops.
