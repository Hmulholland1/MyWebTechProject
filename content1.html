The Milking Process

Dairy goats like cows, are milked twice a day about twelve hours apart to maximize their production. But unlike the typical cow dairy, the goats at Valley View Farm are bred seasonall (in October and November) to “kid” or have their babies in March and April. This is also called “freshening” the goat meaning to refresh their milk supply. Nubians typically have between 1 and four kids, though goats only have two nipples, called teats. Their mammary system is called an udder. At Valley View Farm each doe or dam (the female goat) is “hand bred” to a buck picked out for their genetic potential to improve the qualities of the next generation of milkers – what we call the does in milk. We keep four to six bucks, but sometimes, we artificially inseminate or A.I. a doe if we want to bring in extra special genetics of a long lost or superstar buck. Gestation is 150 days and we have cameras on the herd at kidding time to make sure we can see when they start to come into labor. We want to be with them as they kid to ensure safe delivery of the kids and the health of the mother. We also hand raise the kids by bottle, removing them immediately from their mother. Raising dairy offspring separately from their mother is common in the dairy world. This allows for health management of both milkers and offspring, means the kids will be gentle to human interaction, keeps the udders of the milkers clean and healthy and allows the kids to be sold to new owners early because they can bottle fed without having to wait for weaning age. This saves time and money and space for us. We keep or “retain” about 6-10 doe kids and 1 or 2 bucks each year and sell the rest.

The female goats come into the milking parlor twice a day through a doorway from their pen and up a set of ramps to an elevated platform where they can eat their grain and be milked from behind with an automated bucket milking machine. Each Nubian will milk between a half a gallon and a gallon and half a day. Factors that influence production include age, parity (how many kids they have), genetics, feed, and health. We like to see an average of ¾ a gallon across the whole lactation season which is about 300 days. With 40 goats of various ages and factors, this averages about 6000 gallons of milk that is processed into cheese a year. Before putting the milking machine on the goat’s teats, the udder and teats are washed with a detergent, teats are immersed in an anti-germ “pre-dip”, dried and stripped. After they are milked out, another dip called a post dip is used on each teat to protect them from invading bacteria while the orifice is closing. Normally, the saliva of a baby goat suckling would accomplish this. Each goat only takes a few minutes to “milk out” but the whole process will take about an hour and half two times a day. The milk is poured through a strainer with a filter disc to capture any dirt or debris and into the bulk milk cooling tank which cools 103 degree Fahrenheit milk down to 38 degrees very quickly, preventing the growth of bacteria which will lead to early spoilage of the milk and off flavors. The bulk milk cooling tank is in a room called the milk room, which is a separate room from the milking parlor and the cheese make room. This is according to the State of Massachusetts code for dairy farming and is a way to prevent contamination from the milking parlor on the raw milk and contamination of the finished product from the raw milk.
