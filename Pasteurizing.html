The Pasteurization Process

After 2-3 days of collecting milk, we will make a batch of cheese. The milk is transferred out of the tank and into the pasteurizing/cheese making vat. In our case this is a modified 100 gallon formerly steam heated pasteurizer dating to the 1950s. When we started our dairy, new small scale equipment was not available so we had to modify older equipment to our purposes. The 38 degree milk must be heated up to 145 degrees which is the lowest temperature for legal pasteurization and held for 30 minutes. In addition to killing off bacteria in the milk, we must also heat the air above the milk to 150 degrees to kill off harmful bacteria that might be in the air. Milk can also be pasteurized for a shorter amount of time at higher temperature, but cheese makers prefer to do it at the lower temperature for a longer time believing that it changes the milk less and gives a better curd to the cheese. There is a recording chart that records the rise in temperature, the 30 minutes of pasteurizing and the cool down as well as the air temperature. We chill the milk down by running 32 degree water through pasteurizer.

Depending on the style of cheese we are making, we cool the milk to 70 or 88 degrees. At this point, specific cultures are added depending on the style of cheese we are making. After the cultures have been added, we wait approximately 1 hour, gently stirring the curd, and slowly the cultures active and populating in the milk. After an hour, we add an enzyme called rennet, the amount again dependent upon cheese. The more rennet will cause the milk to coagulate faster. For our soft fresh style cheese, we may add only a few drops, whereas in a harder style we will add up to multiple teaspoons to help it coagulate quicker.
